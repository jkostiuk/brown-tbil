Activity 5.2.12.

Suppose we started using integration by parts to solve the integral \(\displaystyle \int e^x\cos(x)\,dx\) as follows:
\begin{align*} \int e^x\cos(x)\,dx\\ & u=\cos(x) & dv = e^x\,dx\\ & du=-\sin(x) \,dx & v=e^x\\ \int e^x\cos(x)\,dx &= \cos(x)e^x-\int e^x(-\sin(x) \,dx)\\ &= \cos(x)e^x+\int e^x\sin(x) \,dx \end{align*}
Using integration by parts again on the integral \(\displaystyle \int e^x\sin(x)dx\) we get:
\begin{align*} \cos(x)e^x+\int e^x\sin(x)\,dx\\ u=\sin(x) &\ \ dv = e^x\,dx\\ du=\cos(x) \,dx &\ \ v=e^x\\ \cos(x)e^x+\int e^x\sin(x)\,dx &= \cos(x)e^x+\left(e^x\sin(x)-\int e^x\cos{x}\,dx\right)\\ &= \cos(x)e^x+\sin(x)e^x-\int e^x\cos(x) \,dx \end{align*}
Putting this all together:
\begin{equation*} \displaystyle \int e^x\cos(x)dx=\cos(x)e^x+\sin(x)e^x-\int e^x\cos(x)dx. \end{equation*}
Have these computations helped us make progress?
  1. No. We are still no closer to evaluating \(\displaystyle\int e^x\cos(x)dx\text{.}\)
  2. Yes. This equation is helpful in evaluating \(\displaystyle\int e^x\cos(x)dx\text{.}\)
