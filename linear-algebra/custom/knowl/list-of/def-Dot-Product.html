Definition A.3.4.

Given two Euclidean vectors \(\vec{u}=\left[\begin{array}{c}u_{1}\\u_{2} \\\vdots\\u_{n}\end{array}\right]\) and \(\vec{v}=\left[\begin{array}{c}v_{1}\\v_{2} \\\vdots\\v_{n}\end{array}\right]\text{,}\) the dot product of \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) is defined to be:
\begin{equation*} \vec{u}\bullet\vec{v}=\vec{u}=\left[\begin{array}{c}u_{1}\\u_{2} \\\vdots\\u_{n}\end{array}\right]\bullet\vec{v}=\left[\begin{array}{c}v_{1}\\v_{2} \\\vdots\\v_{n}\end{array}\right]=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+\cdots+u_nv_n. \end{equation*}
In particular, note that the dot product of two Euclidean vectors is a real number.
The dot product satisfies the following properties for any Euclidean vectors \(\vec{u},\vec{v}\) and scalar \(c\in\IR\text{:}\)
  1. \(\displaystyle \vec{u}\bullet\vec{v}=\vec{v}\bullet\vec{u}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (\vec{u}+\vec{v})\bullet\vec{w}=\vec{u}\bullet\vec{w}+\vec{v}\bullet\vec{w}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle (c\vec{u})\bullet\vec{v}=c(\vec{u}\bullet\vec{v})=\vec{u}\bullet (c\vec{v})\)
  4. \(\vec{u}\bullet\vec{u}\geq 0\) and, the only way we can have \(\vec{u}\bullet\vec{u}=\vec{0}\) is if \(\vec{u}=\vec{0}.\)
The length (or norm) of a vector \(\vec{u}\in\IR^n\) is denoted by \(||\vec{u}||\) and is given by:
\begin{equation*} ||\vec{u}||=\sqrt{\vec{u}\bullet\vec{u}}=\sqrt{u_1^2+\cdots u_n^2}. \end{equation*}
Interpreting the vector \(\vec{u}\) as an arrow starting at the origin and point in the direction of \(\vec{u}\text{,}\) this norm agrees with our usual notion of length.
Lastly, the dot product can be used to calculate the angle between two vectors. Consider two vectors \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\) and interpret them as arrows with their tails at the origin. If \(\theta\) denotes the angle between these vectors, then it is computed by using the following formula:
\begin{equation*} \cos\theta=\frac{\vec{u}\bullet\vec{v}}{||\vec{u}||\cdot||\vec{v}||}. \end{equation*}
In particular, it follows that the vectors \(\vec{u},\vec{v}\) are perpendicular (or orthogonal) if and only if
\begin{equation*} \vec{u}\bullet\vec{v}=0. \end{equation*}
